You have a great eye for language! The shift from "save one" to "but one" is a perfect example of how the English language has evolved over the last 400 years.

In short: **"Save" is just an old-fashioned way of saying "except" or "but."**

Here is a breakdown of why the King James Version (KJV) uses "save one," and why modern translations changed it to "but one."

### 1. The Definition of "Save" in 1611

When the KJV was translated in 1611, the word "save" had a secondary meaning derived from the Old French word _sauf_ (meaning "safe" or "with the exception of").

In Early Modern English, "save" was commonly used as a preposition or conjunction meaning **"except," "excluding," or "but."**

So, when the KJV says:

> _"none is good, save one, that is, God."_

It translates to modern English as:

> _"none is good, except one, that is, God."_ or _"none is good, but one, that is, God."_

### 2. Other Examples in the KJV

The KJV uses "save" in this exact way all over the place. If you swap it with "except" or "but," the sentences make perfect sense to modern ears:

|Verse|KJV Text|Modern Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**Matthew 17:8**|"...they saw no man, **save** Jesus only."|"...they saw no man, **except** Jesus only."|
|**John 6:46**|"Not that any man hath seen the Father, **save** he which is of God..."|"Not that any man hath seen the Father, **except** he who is from God..."|
|**Galatians 6:14**|"God forbid that I should glory, **save** in the cross..."|"God forbid that I should boast, **except** in the cross..."|

### 3. Why Modern Versions Changed It

Languages change over time. Today, when we hear the word "save," we almost always think of rescuing someone from danger or putting money in a bank.

Because using "save" to mean "except" sounds confusing or clunky to modern readers, newer translations (like the ESV, NASB, or NIV) updated the vocabulary to **"but"** or **"except"** to make the meaning instantly clear.

The copyright status of the King James Version (KJV) and its newer translations can be a bit confusing because it depends entirely on **which version** you are reading and **where** you are located in the world.

Here is the breakdown of how the copyright works for the original KJV, the New King James Version (NKJV), and other modern translations.

---

## 1. The Original King James Version (1611)

For most of the world, the original 1611 KJV (and its standard 1769 text update) is in the **public domain** (Syn, 2001). This means you can freely copy, print, quote, and distribute it without asking anyone for permission. However, there is a major geographical exception:

- **In the United States and most of the world:** The original KJV is completely public domain. You can use it however you like.
    
- **In the United Kingdom:** The KJV is protected by a unique, perpetual **Crown copyright** (Syn, 2001). Under a royal charter, the British Crown controls the rights to print and publish the KJV within the UK. The rights are administered by Cambridge University Press, meaning you need permission to publish commercial editions of the KJV _inside_ the UK.
    

## 2. The New King James Version (NKJV)

If you are referring to the **New King James Version (NKJV)**—which was published by Thomas Nelson in 1982—the answer is **yes, it is strictly copyrighted** (Cohn, 2002).

Because the NKJV modernized the vocabulary, updated the grammar, and re-translated portions using newer manuscript scholarship, it is legally considered a "derivative work" (Cohn, 2002; Naudé & Miller-Naudé, 2022). It possesses enough original creative expression to be protected by standard modern copyright law (Cohn, 2002).

Thomas Nelson (HarperCollins Christian Publishing) owns this copyright. They allow people to quote it freely in small amounts (such as in church bulletins or sermons), but you cannot reprint the entire NKJV text or use it in a commercial book without explicit written permission (Cohn, 2002).

## 3. Other "New" King James Derivatives

Over the years, many publishers have created alternative modern updates to the KJV. Their copyright statuses vary:

- **Modern King James Version (MKJV) / 21st Century King James Version (KJ21):** These are also modern, copyrighted text revisions owned by their respective publishers (Naudé & Miller-Naudé, 2022).
    
- **King James Version 2000 (KJ2000):** This specific update, created by Robert A. Couric, was explicitly placed into the public domain by its author, making it free to use.
